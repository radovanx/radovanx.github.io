LLM Reliability & Hallucinations — Why AI Agents Fail

AI agents are simultaneously capable of extraordinary feats ([[ai-security/glasswing-project|finding 27-year-old security bugs]]) and fundamentally unreliable at multi-step reasoning. Understanding why — and what to do about it — is the central challenge of deploying AI agents in production.

The Math Problem

Vishal Sikka (former SAP CTO, studied under AI founder John McCarthy) and his son Varin published research claiming to mathematically demonstrate that LLMs cannot perform computational tasks beyond relatively low complexity thresholds.

Key claim: “There is no way they can be reliable.” Even at 95% accuracy per step, a 10-step task succeeds only 60% of the time. A 50-step task: 7.7%. This compounding error rate is the fundamental limit — each step multiplies the probability of failure.

OpenAI has acknowledged that hallucinations persist despite advances and model accuracy will never reach 100%. Companies that downsized to replace workers with AI agents discovered they couldn’t sustain the same output quality.

Hallucination — The Core Failure Mode

When models generate plausible but false information, it undermines every downstream decision. This isn’t a bug to be fixed — it’s an emergent property of how language models work (statistical pattern completion, not knowledge retrieval).

No single approach solves the problem. The practical path: combine multiple strategies and design systems that tolerate failures.

The Guardrail Argument

Despite fundamental limitations, Sikka acknowledged that external guardrails and supporting components around models can overcome inherent LLM weaknesses. The model alone isn’t enough, but the system around it might be.

This maps to the [[ai-agents/tool-search|tool search]] pattern — agents that dynamically discover and use external tools rather than relying on internal knowledge. And to [[ai-agents/persistent-agent-memory|persistent memory]] — agents that verify against accumulated knowledge rather than re-deriving answers.

The Paradox

Glasswing ([[ai-security/glasswing-project]]) deploys AI to find security vulnerabilities with superhuman accuracy. Yet the same technology can’t reliably do multi-step math. The resolution: AI excels at pattern matching at scale but fails at sequential reasoning. Finding a vulnerability = pattern matching across millions of code paths. Solving a 10-step problem = sequential reasoning where each step depends on the last.

Design your agent architecture around this distinction.

Implications for This Wiki

This wiki itself is an example of the guardrail approach. Instead of asking an LLM to derive answers from raw sources every time (RAG — unreliable), we pre-compile knowledge into structured pages with cross-references. The LLM still hallucinates during compilation, but humans curate the sources, review the wiki, and the structured format makes errors visible and correctable.
